A Weekend in Cape Coast: Travel Diary

January 22, 2026 · 8 min read

I've been meaning to write this post for weeks, but every time I sat down to start, I got overwhelmed by how much I wanted to say. Our weekend trip to Cape Coast was one of those experiences that stays with you — the kind that shifts something inside you and makes you see your own country with fresh eyes. So grab a cup of tea, get comfortable, and let me take you along.

The Road Trip from Accra

We left Accra early on a Friday morning — and by early, I mean 5 AM, because if you've ever tried to leave Accra after 7 AM on a Friday, you know it's a three-hour task just getting past Kasoa. My friend Ama drove, I was in charge of the playlist (a sacred responsibility), and our friend Nana was in the backseat with the snacks. We had a cooler full of FanIce, chin chin, and more bofrot than three people should reasonably consume.

The drive from Accra to Cape Coast is about three and a half hours if the road is kind to you. We took the coastal road, and once you get past Winneba, the scenery starts to change. The air gets saltier, the landscape opens up, and you can catch glimpses of the Atlantic through the palm trees. We had the windows down, Amaarae playing on the speakers, and for the first time in weeks, I felt my shoulders drop from my ears. There's something about a road trip that reminds you that life exists beyond your daily commute.

We stopped in Mankessim for some waakye from a roadside vendor, and let me tell you — it was the kind of waakye that makes you close your eyes and whisper "thank you, Lord." Perfectly stewed beans, shito that had just the right kick, and fried fish that was crispy all the way through. Road trip food just hits different.

Cape Coast Castle

We arrived at Cape Coast Castle on Friday afternoon, and I want to be honest with you — nothing prepares you for this experience. I had visited once as a child on a school trip, but going back as an adult was entirely different.

Cape Coast Castle is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, one of the many slave castles along Ghana's coastline. It was built by the Swedish in the 1600s and later used by the British as a major hub in the transatlantic slave trade. Walking through those walls, you are walking through one of the most painful chapters in human history.

The tour guide led us through the dungeons where enslaved Africans were held — hundreds of people packed into dark, airless rooms for weeks or months before being shipped across the Atlantic. The floor of the female dungeon is still uneven from layers of compacted human waste that were never fully removed. There are scratch marks on the walls. The "Door of No Return" — the final doorway through which enslaved people passed before boarding the ships — opens directly onto the sea.

I stood at that door for a long time. The Atlantic stretched out endlessly before me, beautiful and terrible at the same time. I thought about the millions of people who passed through that doorway and never came back. I thought about their families, their languages, their names that were stripped away. And I thought about the fact that I am standing here, on this soil, free and whole, because of their resilience. It was overwhelming. Ama and I held hands and cried quietly. Nana stood with his hand on the wall, silent.

I share this not to be heavy, but because I think every Ghanaian — and every person — should visit Cape Coast Castle at least once. It is a place of immense sorrow, but also of remembrance and reverence. We owe it to our ancestors to bear witness to what happened here.

Kakum National Park

On Saturday morning, we drove about 30 minutes north to Kakum National Park for the famous canopy walkway. If Cape Coast Castle grounded me in history, Kakum lifted me into the sky — literally.

The canopy walkway is a series of seven suspension bridges hanging about 40 meters above the rainforest floor. Let me be clear: I am afraid of heights. When the guide said "40 meters," my brain translated that to "certain death" and my legs turned to jelly. But I had come this far, and there was no way I was letting Nana have this over me.

The first bridge was terrifying. It swings with every step, the wooden planks creak beneath your feet, and when you look down, you see nothing but dense green canopy and the occasional bird swooping below you. I gripped the rope railing so hard my knuckles turned white. But by the third bridge, something shifted. I loosened my grip. I looked up instead of down. And the view — oh, the view. An endless ocean of green treetops stretching to the horizon, the morning mist still clinging to the valleys, birds calling from every direction. I felt small in the most beautiful way possible.

We spent the rest of the morning hiking one of the nature trails. The forest was alive — butterflies the size of my hand, tree roots that looked like ancient sculptures, the sound of unseen creatures rustling in the undergrowth. Our guide pointed out medicinal plants that local healers still use, and told us stories about the forest that felt almost magical. By the time we emerged, sweaty and covered in mosquito bites, I felt like I'd had a full-body reset.

The Best Local Food

Cape Coast's food scene is underrated, and I will stand by that. Here are the spots we tried and loved:

  • Castle Restaurant — Right near the castle, with views of the ocean. We had grilled tilapia with banku and the most incredible pepper sauce. The fish was so fresh it practically swam onto our plates.
  • Oasis Beach Resort restaurant — We went here for Saturday lunch. I had their coconut rice with grilled prawns and it was absolutely divine. Sitting by the beach, feet in the sand, eating perfectly seasoned prawns — that's what I call living.
  • A street vendor near UCC campus — I don't know her name, but the kelewele lady near the University of Cape Coast campus deserves a Michelin star. Perfectly spiced, perfectly fried, served in a brown paper bag with groundnuts on the side. We went back twice.

Elmina Beach at Sunset

On Saturday evening, we drove to Elmina — about 15 minutes from Cape Coast — and found a quiet stretch of beach to watch the sunset. The fishing boats were coming in for the day, their colorful painted hulls bobbing in the golden light. Fishermen were pulling in their nets, children were playing in the shallow waves, and the whole scene looked like a painting.

We spread a cloth on the sand, shared a bottle of Alvaro (the pear flavor, because we're civilized), and watched the sun melt into the Atlantic. The sky turned from gold to peach to deep violet. Nobody said much. We didn't need to. Sometimes the most meaningful moments are the ones where you just sit and breathe and let the beauty wash over you.

I took about fifty photos, but none of them captured what it actually felt like to be there. Some experiences are just for you to hold in your memory.

Our Hotel

We stayed at a lovely little guesthouse in Cape Coast town — nothing fancy, but clean, comfortable, and run by the warmest couple who made us breakfast every morning. Fresh bread, eggs, fruit, and the strongest coffee I've ever had. The rooms had mosquito nets and ceiling fans, and from our window, we could hear the ocean. It cost about 250 cedis per night per room, which felt like a steal for how charming it was. Sometimes the boutique guesthouses with character are so much better than the big, impersonal hotels.

What I Packed

For anyone planning a similar trip, here's what I packed in my weekend bag:

  • Comfortable walking shoes (I wore my trusty white sneakers — they were not white when I came home)
  • A light cotton dress for the castle visit and Elmina beach
  • Workout leggings and a breathable top for the Kakum hike
  • Swimsuit (always pack one, even if you think you won't need it)
  • Sunscreen, sunscreen, sunscreen — SPF 50, and I still reapplied multiple times
  • Insect repellent (Kakum's mosquitoes are no joke)
  • A light cardigan for the evenings
  • My journal and Bible for morning devotional time
  • A portable phone charger — essential for all the photos you'll be taking
  • Cash in small denominations (not everywhere accepts mobile money outside Accra)

Travel Tips for Visiting Cape Coast

A few things I wish I'd known or that might help you plan your own trip:

  • Leave Accra early — Beat the Kasoa traffic. 5-6 AM departure is ideal for a smooth drive.
  • Visit the castle first thing in the morning — It's less crowded, cooler, and you'll have more time to absorb the experience without feeling rushed.
  • Wear comfortable shoes everywhere — The castle has uneven floors, and Kakum involves real hiking. Leave the cute sandals at the hotel.
  • Bring water — Staying hydrated is crucial, especially at Kakum. Bring at least two liters per person.
  • Hire a local guide — At both the castle and Kakum, the guides add so much depth to the experience. Tip them generously — they deserve it.
  • Be respectful at the castle — This is a sacred site of remembrance. Keep your voice low, listen carefully, and approach the experience with the solemnity it deserves.
  • Stay an extra night if you can — Two nights gives you a much more relaxed pace. One night feels rushed.
  • Try the local food — Don't default to hotel restaurants. The street food and small local spots are where the real magic is.

Final Thoughts

This weekend reminded me of something I sometimes forget living in the bustle of Accra: Ghana is breathtakingly beautiful, deeply historical, and full of stories waiting to be experienced. We spend so much time dreaming about international travel that we overlook the treasures in our own backyard.

Cape Coast gave me history that humbled me, nature that awed me, food that delighted me, and sunsets that healed me. It was exactly the kind of trip my soul needed — a reminder to slow down, look around, and be grateful for this incredible country I call home.

If you haven't visited Cape Coast yet, please go. Not just as a tourist destination, but as a pilgrimage. Walk through the castle. Stand at the Door of No Return. Climb above the rainforest canopy. Watch the sun set over Elmina. Let Ghana break your heart open and put it back together, fuller than before.

xo, Roberta
