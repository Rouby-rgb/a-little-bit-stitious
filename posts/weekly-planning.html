How I Plan My Week for Maximum Productivity

January 10, 2026 · 5 min read

I used to start every Monday morning in a panic. I'd open my laptop, stare at my to-do list, and feel instantly overwhelmed by everything I needed to do — blog posts to write, content to plan, workouts to schedule, Bible study to keep up with, a business to build. It was chaos. Then I started planning my weeks intentionally, and honestly? It changed everything. Let me show you exactly how I do it.

My Sunday Planning Ritual

Every Sunday evening, usually around 7 PM after church and dinner, I sit down for what I call my "weekly reset." I make a cup of tea (currently obsessed with honey and lemon), light a candle, put on some soft worship music, and spend about 45 minutes planning the entire week ahead. It's become one of my favorite rituals — it calms my mind and helps me enter the week with clarity instead of chaos.

I start by reviewing the past week. What did I accomplish? What got carried over? What can I let go of entirely? This reflection piece is important because it prevents the never-ending to-do list syndrome where tasks just pile up week after week without ever being evaluated. Some things that felt urgent last Monday genuinely don't matter by Sunday, and that's okay.

The Tools I Use

I've tried so many planners and apps, and honestly, I've landed on a hybrid system that works beautifully for me:

  • A physical weekly planner — I use a simple, undated planner with a weekly spread. There's something about writing things by hand that makes them feel more real and helps me remember them. I use different colored pens for different categories (more on that below).
  • Google Calendar — For time-blocking and appointments. I color-code everything so I can see at a glance how my week is balanced. If my calendar is all one color, something is off.
  • Notes app on my phone — For quick brain dumps throughout the week. Whenever a random task or idea pops into my head, I jot it here so it doesn't live rent-free in my brain. During Sunday planning, I sort through these notes and assign them to the right day.

How I Categorize My Tasks

This was the game-changer for me. Instead of writing one massive to-do list, I break everything into five categories. Each category gets its own color in my planner:

  • Blog & Content (pink) — Writing posts, editing photos, scheduling social media, engaging with my community, brainstorming ideas.
  • Business (purple) — Emails, collaborations, brand partnerships, financial tracking, strategy planning. Balancing blogging with entrepreneurship means both need dedicated space.
  • Fitness & Health (green) — Workout sessions, meal prep, grocery shopping, rest days. Yes, rest days are scheduled. They're not optional.
  • Faith & Growth (blue) — Bible study, devotionals, church commitments, journaling, books I'm reading. This is the foundation everything else is built on.
  • Personal & Fun (orange) — Friend dates, family time, self-care, hobbies, Netflix nights. Because if your planner doesn't include joy, what's the point?

When I look at my weekly spread and see a healthy mix of colors, I know my week is balanced. When it's dominated by one color (usually pink or purple, if I'm being honest), I know I need to redistribute and make room for the other areas of my life.

Time-Blocking: My Secret Weapon

I time-block every weekday in 2-hour chunks. Here's what a typical day looks like for me:

  • 5:30 – 6:30 AM — Morning devotional, prayer, journaling. This is sacred time and nothing touches it.
  • 6:30 – 7:30 AM — Workout or walk.
  • 8:00 – 10:00 AM — Deep work block. This is when I write blog posts, work on big projects, or do anything that requires serious focus. Phone goes on Do Not Disturb.
  • 10:00 – 12:00 PM — Business tasks — emails, calls, admin work.
  • 12:00 – 1:00 PM — Lunch and rest. I actually eat away from my desk and take a real break.
  • 1:00 – 3:00 PM — Content creation — photos, social media, engagement.
  • 3:00 – 5:00 PM — Flexible block for overflow tasks, personal errands, or creative projects.
  • Evening — Personal time. Dinner, reading, family, rest.

The beauty of time-blocking is that it removes decision fatigue. I don't waste mental energy wondering what to do next because it's already decided. I just look at my calendar and execute.

My Non-Negotiables

These are the things that happen every single week, no matter how busy I am. They're blocked in my calendar first, before anything else:

  • Morning devotional — Every day, no exceptions. Even if it's just 15 minutes, I start with God.
  • Movement — At least 4 days of intentional exercise. My body and mind need it.
  • One blog post — Consistency is what grows a blog. One post, every single week.
  • Sabbath rest — Sunday is for church, family, and rest. I don't work on Sundays (except the evening planning session, which honestly feels more like self-care than work).
  • Reading — At least 30 minutes a day. It feeds my mind and my creativity.

How I Handle Overwhelm

Even with the best planning system, overwhelm still creeps in. Here's what I do when it hits:

First, I stop and breathe. Literally. Three deep breaths. It sounds too simple to work, but it interrupts the panic spiral every time.

Then I look at my list and ask myself: "What are the three most important things today?" Not ten. Not seven. Three. I circle those three things and give myself permission to let the rest wait. Nine times out of ten, the world doesn't end because I didn't answer that email until tomorrow.

I also give myself grace. I used to beat myself up on Fridays if I didn't complete everything on my list. Now I celebrate what I did accomplish instead of fixating on what I didn't. Progress over perfection, always.

Advice for Busy Women Who Want to Be More Intentional

If you're reading this feeling like your weeks just happen to you instead of for you, here are a few things I'd say:

  • Start small — You don't need a perfect system on day one. Start by writing down your top 3 priorities for tomorrow. That's it. Build from there.
  • Plan on paper first — There's something powerful about physically writing your plans. It engages your brain differently than typing.
  • Protect your mornings — However your morning starts sets the tone for everything. Guard that time fiercely.
  • Say no more often — Every yes to something is a no to something else. Be intentional about what gets your energy.
  • Include rest in your plan — Rest is not lazy. Rest is productive. Schedule it like you would a meeting.

Final Thoughts

Planning isn't about controlling every minute of your life. It's about being intentional with the time God has given you. It's about making sure the things that matter most — your faith, your health, your relationships, your dreams — actually get space in your week instead of being pushed aside by whatever feels most urgent in the moment.

Your week is going to happen whether you plan it or not. Why not make it happen on your terms?
